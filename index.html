01 / Introduction

As Large Language Models assume autonomous roles in healthcare, education, and policy, understanding their consistent behavioral tendencies—their "personality"—is no longer optional.

While extensive research evaluates capabilities and safety, we lack systematic characterizations of the decision styles and moral preferences that shape how models engage with open-ended queries. This project bridges this gap by subjecting LLMs to standardized psychometric assessments designed for humans.

Crucially, we look beyond the text. By employing mechanistic interpretability, we examine the internal probability distributions that precede a response, revealing how models weigh options, resolve uncertainty, and prioritize values before they "speak."

02 / Experimental Setup

We adapted six distinct psychological instruments, translating human psychometric standards into machine-readable prompts to probe the latent space.

Personality

IPIP-BFFM

The "Big Five" Factor Markers. A comprehensive 50-item inventory assessing the cardinal dimensions of Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism.

Dark Traits

Short Dark Triad

Investigates malevolent behavioral tendencies. Specifically measures Machiavellianism (strategic manipulation), Narcissism (ego-reinforcement), and Psychopathy (affective callousness).

Ethics (High Ambiguity)

MoralChoice

Complex ethical dilemmas characterized by competing principles (e.g., utilitarianism vs. deontology) where no clear "correct" answer exists, revealing value prioritization.

Ethics (Low Ambiguity)

MoralChoice Baseline

Unambiguous control scenarios designed to verify if the model can correctly identify clear moral violations versus standard social conventions.

Safety

Compliance

A stress test for the boundary between helpfulness and harm prevention. Evaluates the model's refusal rate when presented with ostensibly unsafe or unethical requests.

Game Theory

Social Value Orientation

Determines the magnitude of concern for others. Classifies the model as prosocial (cooperative), individualistic (self-maximizing), or competitive.

03 / Methodology

Mechanistic Interpretability

We moved beyond black-box analysis by implementing a multi-method framework. This allows us to trace the "thought process" of the model layer by layer.

  • 01
    Causal Tracing via Activation Patching

    Using counterfactual interventions to pinpoint exactly which layers cause the decision, distinguishing them from layers that merely correlate.

  • 02
    Direct Logit Attribution (DLA)

    Decomposing the final output into its architectural sources: does the decision come from an Attention Head (context) or an MLP layer (knowledge)?

Custom Engineering

Unlike standard studies relying on high-level libraries, we architected a custom interpretability framework from scratch. This allowed for precise, low-level intervention during the forward pass without abstraction overhead.

Distributed (AFM)

Ethical reasoning is spread across early and late layers. This makes the model robust to single-layer damage (ablation) but results in lower overall confidence and accuracy.

Concentrated (LLaMA)

"Winner-take-all" dynamics. 89% of computation happens at the very first and very last layers. This yields high accuracy but creates critical vulnerabilities where attacking a single layer can flip the decision.

05 / Conclusion

Our findings establish that ethical reasoning in LLMs is not a diffuse property but localizes to specific computational stages. We identified a fundamental trade-off: Concentrated architectures (like LLaMA) are more accurate but fragile, while Distributed architectures (like AFM) are robust but less decisive.

Perhaps most concerningly, we found persistent alignment failures (21-32%) across both models. Mechanistic analysis reveals these are not due to "confusion"—the models are often highly confident in their unethical choices. This suggests misalignment is deeply encoded in the representation space, meaning simply scaling up the model size is unlikely to solve the problem.

Rigorous behavioral profiling combined with mechanistic interpretability is essential for building AI systems that reliably serve human values.
